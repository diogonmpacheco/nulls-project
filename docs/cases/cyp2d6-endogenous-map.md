# CYP2D6 Endogenous Substrate Map

This note starts the source map for CYP2D6 beyond pharmacogenomics.

The important claim is not:

> CYP2D6 null automatically means low serotonin, low dopamine, or a psychiatric diagnosis.

The important claim is:

> CYP2D6 has source-linked candidate endogenous routes. A true null may remove local enzyme capacity and shift pathway load to other synthesis, clearance, transport, or degradation routes.

That is exactly the kind of missing-function question The Nulls Project should preserve.

## Why This Matters

Pharmacogenomics usually asks whether a drug changes exposure, activation, or toxicity.

The systemic-null question is broader:

- what endogenous molecules can CYP2D6 process?
- where is CYP2D6 normally expressed or functional?
- what happens when that local route is absent from birth?
- which pathways compensate?
- which external substrates compete for the same missing function?
- which claims are human evidence, animal evidence, or biochemical plausibility?

## First Pathway Rows

### 5-Methoxytryptamine To Serotonin

Human CYP2D6 can convert 5-methoxytryptamine to serotonin by O-demethylation. Later animal brain work supports brain CYP2D catalytic competence for serotonin formation and a melatonin / 5-methoxytryptamine / serotonin route.

Null question:

> Does inherited CYP2D6 absence remove a backup serotonin-regeneration route in CYP2D6-expressing compartments?

Boundary:

This should not be simplified to "CYP2D6 null equals serotonin deficiency." Classical tryptophan-derived serotonin synthesis and other regulatory pathways remain central. The atlas should model a missing route and possible flux shift, not a deterministic neurotransmitter level.

### Tyramine To Dopamine

Human CYP2D6 can hydroxylate tyramine to dopamine. Tyramine can be endogenous and diet-derived, so this route is a bridge between internal trace-amine handling and external exposure.

Null question:

> Does inherited CYP2D6 absence reduce an alternative dopamine-formation route and shift tyramine handling toward monoamine oxidase or other clearance routes?

Boundary:

This is not the same as proving global dopamine deficiency. It is a pathway-capacity question.

### Anandamide / Endocannabinoid Metabolism

Polymorphic human CYP2D6 can metabolize the endocannabinoid anandamide.

Null question:

> Does CYP2D6 absence change endocannabinoid metabolite ratios in brain or other CYP2D6-expressing compartments?

Boundary:

This is currently weaker than the medication pharmacokinetic evidence. It belongs in the atlas as a candidate endogenous route, not as a diagnosis or symptom claim.

### External CNS-Active Substrates

The same missing function can also affect external substances: medicines, diet-derived molecules, and xenobiotics. For each substrate, the atlas should ask:

- does CYP2D6 clear the parent?
- does CYP2D6 create an active metabolite?
- does CYP2D6 create an inactive metabolite?
- does the missing route increase parent accumulation?
- does it lower metabolite formation?
- do other enzymes compensate?
- does this happen in liver, brain, barrier tissue, or another compartment?

This is the bridge between endogenous routes and medication/exposure cases such as solanidine.

## Evidence Grading Rule

Every row should separate:

- human enzyme in vitro evidence;
- animal brain in vivo evidence;
- human brain expression evidence;
- human brain functional evidence;
- human phenotype evidence;
- drug/external-substrate evidence;
- null-specific evidence.

The atlas should treat human null-specific phenotype evidence as the highest bar. Biochemical and animal-brain evidence are still valuable, but they should be labeled as pathway evidence rather than clinical certainty.
